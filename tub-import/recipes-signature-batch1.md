## spaghetti-carbonara

  popular_in: "Rome — the trattoria classic; beloved worldwide"
  famous_for: "The four-ingredient pasta that proves restraint is a flex — and the gateway to the Roman pasta canon"
  consists_of: "Spaghetti, guanciale, egg yolks, Pecorino Romano and a storm of black pepper — bound by the pasta's own cooking water"
  name_origin: "From carbonaro — 'charcoal burner'. The black pepper looks like coal dust; the dish was a hearty meal for Apennine workers."
  why_loved: "Ready in the time the water takes to boil; rich, salty, silky — the pasta that turns a weeknight into an event"
  method_detail: "Stovetop — emulsion (egg + cheese + starch water + rendered fat)"
pairing:
  drink: "Frascati or Verdicchio — crisp, dry, Roman"
  why: "Their clean acidity cuts the richness and stands up to the pepper without overpowering the dish"
  aperitif: "Aperol Spritz"
```

### Brief
The whole cook happens in the last ninety seconds, so have everything ready before the pasta
drains. You'll render the guanciale to a crisp, boil the pasta shy of al dente, then — **off the
heat** — toss it with an egg-and-cheese paste loosened with starchy pasta water until it turns
to glossy silk. The single thing that ruins carbonara is heat: too much and the eggs scramble.
Keep the pan off the flame for the toss and you cannot lose.

- **Time:** ~20 min active.
- **Serves:** 2 (scales to 1 / 4 / 6).
- **Kit:** large pot, heavy frying pan, tongs, a mug for pasta water.

### Ingredients
**The pasta**
- 200 g dried spaghetti (or rigatoni)

**The cure**
- 100 g guanciale, diced small — *SA note: pancetta or good streaky bacon work if guanciale is scarce*

**The sauce**
- 3 large egg yolks + 1 whole egg (or 4 yolks for richer)
- 50 g Pecorino Romano, finely grated, plus extra to finish
- Coarsely cracked black pepper, generous

### Timeline
- **T+00:00** — large pot of well-salted water on high; prep the egg-cheese paste.
- **T+06:00** — guanciale into a cold pan, medium-low; render slowly to crisp-gold. Kill the heat.
- **T+08:00** — pasta in; cook to **1 minute shy** of al dente.
- **T+17:00** — reserve a mug of starchy water; drain.
- **T+18:00** — pasta into the guanciale pan (off heat); add paste, splash in hot water, toss hard.
- **T+20:00** — loosen to a cream, plate, more pepper and pecorino. **Eat immediately.**

### Steps
1. **Water & the paste.** Salt the water like the sea. Whisk yolks, egg, pecorino and a heavy
   grind of pepper into a thick paste. *Why: this paste is the entire sauce — and the water is
   salted because the pasta is your only other seasoning route.*
2. **Render the cure.** Guanciale into a **cold** pan, then medium-low. Let the fat melt out
   slowly until the meat is crisp and amber. Take it off the heat. *Why: a slow render gives
   you crisp meat and a pool of flavourful fat to emulsify into; a hot start toughens it.*
3. **Cook shy.** Pasta to one minute under al dente; scoop out a big mug of cloudy water before
   draining. *Why: it finishes in the sauce, and that starchy water is the emulsifier that makes
   it silky rather than claggy.*
4. **Temper & toss — off the heat.** Tip the pasta into the guanciale pan (off the flame, cooled
   a beat). Add the paste, a splash of hot pasta water, and toss violently with tongs. *Why:
   off-heat stops the eggs scrambling; the water's heat and starch turn the paste into a
   flowing, glossy sauce.*
5. **Loosen & plate.** Add more pasta water until it moves like cream. Plate, crack more pepper,
   shower with pecorino. *Why: carbonara waits for no one — the sauce is perfect for about
   ninety seconds, so have the wine poured and sit down.*

## crispy-skin-salmon-brown-butter-capers

```yaml

  consists_of: "A skin-on salmon fillet, flaky salt, a little neutral oil, butter, capers and lemon — and the patience to leave it alone"
  name_origin: "Self-descriptive: the skin is fried to a crackle and the butter is browned to a nutty beurre noisette"
  why_loved: "One pan, ten minutes, looks like a chef cooked it — and the crispy skin is the whole point"
  method_detail: "Stovetop — pan-fry (cold-start, crisp skin) + beurre noisette (browned-butter sauce)"
pairing:
  drink: "Sancerre or a crisp Sauvignon Blanc"
  why: "Bright acidity cuts the butter and echoes the capers' brine; a cold Chablis works just as well"
  aperitif: "A dry gin martini, very cold"
```

### Brief
This is a lesson in leaving things alone. The skin has to be bone-dry and started in a **cold
pan** so it renders slowly into glass; you press it flat, then don't touch it for six minutes.
The centre stays blushing because the skin side does all the work. Finish by browning butter in
the same pan with capers and lemon — *beurre noisette* — and spooning it over. Expect patience,
then speed.

- **Time:** ~12 min active + 10 min salting the skin.
- **Serves:** 2 (scales to 1 / 4 / 6).
- **Kit:** heavy or non-stick pan, fish slice/spatula, spoon for basting.

### Ingredients
**The fish**
- 2 × salmon fillets, skin on, scaled and pin-boned, ~180 g each
- Flaky sea salt

**To cook**
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (canola/grapeseed)

**The sauce (beurre noisette)**
- 60 g butter
- 1½ tbsp capers, drained
- ½ lemon
- Fresh dill, optional

### Timeline
- **T−10:00** — pat skin bone-dry; salt it generously; leave to draw moisture.
- **T+00:00** — oil in a **cold** pan, fish skin-down; medium heat; press flat 30 sec.
- **T+06:00** — don't move it; cook skin-down until deep gold, flesh opaque ⅔ up.
- **T+07:30** — flip; 60–90 sec flesh-side to set the centre.
- **T+09:00** — fish out; butter in, foams then browns; capers, lemon.
- **T+10:00** — spoon over; plate skin-up; dill.

### Steps
1. **Dry & season the skin.** Pat the skin dry with paper towel — several times — until it's
   matt, not glossy. Salt it well ten minutes ahead; salt the flesh lightly. *Why: moisture is
   the enemy of crisp skin; salting early draws water out and seasons from the start.*
2. **Cold-pan start.** Oil in a cold pan, fish **skin-down**, then medium heat. Press gently for
   thirty seconds so it doesn't curl. *Why: starting cold renders the skin slowly and evenly into
   glass; a hot start curls it and steams it soft.*
3. **Don't touch it.** Leave it skin-down about six minutes, until the skin is deep gold and the
   flesh is opaque two-thirds of the way up. *Why: patience is what crisps the skin — you can
   literally watch the colour climb the side of the fillet.*
4. **Flip & finish.** Turn it over for 60–90 seconds, just to set the top. The centre stays
   blushing. *Why: the skin side cooked the fish; the flip only finishes the flesh gently so it
   stays moist.*
5. **Brown butter & capers.** Lift the fish out. In the same pan, the butter will foam, then
   turn golden and smell of hazelnut. Toss in the capers and a good squeeze of lemon. *Why:
   beurre noisette with capers and lemon is the classic sharp, nutty finish for fish — the
   browned butter is the sauce.*
6. **Spoon over, plate skin-up.** Pour the foaming butter over the fish, keeping the skin on
   top. Dill if you like. *Why: skin-up keeps the crackle intact; the butter dresses the flesh
   beneath.*

## roast-chicken-lemon-garlic-herbs

  method_detail: "Oven — roast (high-heat crisp, then steady, then a long rest)"

  drink: "Pinot Noir or a Cru Beaujolais"
  why: "Bright red fruit and gentle tannin suit the bird without overpowering it; a lightly oaked Chardonnay is the white answer"
  aperitif: "A glass of Champagne — it sets the evening, and the bird won't keep you waiting"
```

### Brief
Fifteen minutes of you, then the oven does the work. The secrets are dry skin, butter *under*
the skin, a hot start for colour, and a generous rest. Stuff the cavity with lemon, garlic and
herbs so it perfumes from within. Expect a golden, juicy bird with crisp skin and lemony pan
juices — the smell alone earns the evening.

- **Time:** ~15 min active + ~60 min roasting + 15 min rest.
- **Serves:** 4 (scales; a larger bird for 6).
- **Kit:** roasting tray, instant-read thermometer (kind), carving board.

### Ingredients
**The bird**
- 1 × whole free-range chicken, ~1.6 kg

**The butter**
- 80 g butter, softened
- Flaky sea salt and black pepper
- 4 sprigs thyme + 1 sprig rosemary, leaves picked and chopped

**The aromatics**
- 1 lemon, halved
- 1 head garlic, halved across the equator
- Extra thyme/rosemary sprigs

**To serve**
- 1 tbsp olive oil (to rub)
- Optional: 2 onions or lemons, quartered, for the pan

### Timeline
- **T−(hrs)** — if you can, leave the bird uncovered in the fridge a few hours to dry the skin.
- **T+00:00** — oven 220 °C; prep butter, loosen skin, stuff cavity.
- **T+02:00** — into the hot oven, breast-up, 20 min.
- **T+22:00** — turn down to 200 °C; roast on.
- **T+50:00** — baste once with pan juices.
- **T+60–70:00** — done when the thigh reads 74 °C and juices run clear.
- **T+70:00** — rest, loosely tented, 15 min. Carve, pour over juices.

### Steps
1. **Dry the bird.** Pat it dry inside and out — and, if time allows, leave it uncovered in the
   fridge for a few hours. *Why: dry skin is crisp skin; a fridge-dried bird is the professional
   route to glass-like skin.*
2. **Butter under the skin.** Mix softened butter with salt, pepper and chopped herbs. Ease your
   fingers under the breast skin to loosen it and smear the butter *directly onto the meat*; rub
   the rest over the outside. Stuff the cavity with lemon, garlic and herb sprigs. *Why: butter
   under the skin bastes the breast from within and crisps the skin above; cavity aromatics
   perfume the bird from the inside out.*
3. **Hot start.** Into a 220 °C oven, breast-up, for twenty minutes. *Why: the initial blast
   sets and colours the skin before the long, steady roast.*
4. **Turn down & roast.** Drop to 200 °C and roast about fifteen to twenty minutes per 500 g,
   basting once. *Why: the lower heat cooks the joints through without burning the skin; basting
   adds gloss and carries flavour over the surface.*
5. **Check & rest.** It's done at 74 °C in the thickest part of the thigh, with clear juices.
   Rest it, loosely tented with foil, for fifteen minutes. *Why: resting lets the juices settle
   back into the meat — carve immediately and the breast runs dry.*
6. **Carve & pour.** Carve at the table if you like the theatre, and pour the lemony pan juices
   over everything. *Why: those juices are liquid gold — spooning them over is the difference
   between good and unforgettable.*
```
