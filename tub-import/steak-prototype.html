Pan-Seared
Steak · Butter
& Thyme

A great steak is less recipe than ritual. Salt it early, let the pan get properly hot, baste in foaming butter and thyme — and cook it for someone you'd like to keep talking to.

Provenance

Where it comes from, and why it matters

Most popular in
The American steakhouse and the French bistro — and, in truth, any kitchen with a heavy pan and a properly hot flame.
Known for
The ritual that turns a single cut into a restaurant plate: a deep, dark crust, a rose-pink centre, and a finish of butter basted straight from the pan.
Consists of
A thick sirloin or ribeye, flaky salt and cracked pepper, a little neutral oil for the sear, then butter, thyme and garlic to baste. Four elements, done deliberately.
On the name
Self-descriptive — pan-seared for the crust, basted in butter and thyme. The French have a word for the baste: arroser, to shower with the pan's own foaming fat.
Why it's loved
Minimal ingredients, maximal theatre. Done well, it's the dish that makes a quiet evening feel like an occasion — and it cooks in the time it takes to pour the wine.
Method
⬢ Stovetop — sear + baste
Before you start

The brief

Before the heat goes on, know the shape of the cook. It's loud and fast at the pan, then quiet while it rests — the crust forms in minutes, the magic is in the baste, and the rest is non-negotiable.

  • ◆Temper & salt early. Pull the steak from the fridge, salt it, let it sit. This is the step most people skip and shouldn't.
  • ◆A screaming-hot pan. The crust only forms if the pan is properly hot before the steak goes in.
  • ◆Baste to finish. Lower the heat, tilt the pan, and spoon foaming butter over the top until it smells of thyme and garlic.
  • ◆Rest, then slice. Five to eight minutes off the heat — slice now and the board floods.

Step by step

Guided cook

Temper & salt

Season the steak generously with flaky salt 30–45 minutes ahead and leave it out to lose its chill. Just before cooking, pat it completely dry.

WHY · Salt seasons deep and draws surface moisture for a darker crust; tempering lets the steak cook evenly instead of seizing cold.
done

Dry & pepper

Pat the steak bone-dry with paper towel, then crack black pepper over both sides — after the salt, not before.

WHY · Moisture is the enemy of crust. Pepper goes on late so it flavours the meat without scorching in the hot pan.
done

Get the pan properly hot

Heavy pan over high heat until a tiny drop of water skitters across the surface and vanishes. Only then add a thin film of neutral oil.

WHY · A screaming-hot pan is the only way to build real Maillard crust before the centre overcooks. Patience here, speed next.
done

Sear — and don't touch it

Lay the steak in, away from you. Leave it alone for 2 minutes. Flip, 2 minutes more. Use tongs to hold it on its fat cap for 30–60 seconds to render the edge.

WHY · Moving the steak tears the crust as it forms. Let the pan do the work; the steak releases when it's ready.
done

Baste in foaming butter

Drop the heat. Add the butter, thyme and smashed garlic. Tilt the pan toward you and, with a spoon, shower the foaming butter over the steak for 60–90 seconds, until it smells of thyme.

WHY · Arroser — basting gently cooks the top and perfumes the fat, giving you an even, glossy finish and that restaurant aroma.
done

Rest

Move the steak to a warm plate and rest it, loosely tented with foil, for 5–8 minutes. Don't skip this and don't slice early.

WHY · Resting lets the muscle fibres relax and the juices redistribute. Cut now and every drop runs onto the board instead of staying in the meat.
done

Slice & finish

Slice against the grain, on a board. Finish with flaky salt, a knob of butter melting over the slices, and a squeeze of lemon if you like.

WHY · Slicing across the grain shortens the fibres for tenderness; the final salt and butter are the difference between good and unforgettable.
done
To drink

The pairing

With the steak

Malbec

Mendoza, Argentina · full-bodied red

Dark fruit, a lick of spice and soft tannins that cut through the butter and stand up to the char. Served just under room temperature, it's the classic foil to a basted steak — generous, like the meal.

Beforehand

Negroni

Equal parts gin · Campari · sweet vermouth

Bitter and bright, it wakes the palate and sets the evening's tone while the steak tempers. Stir it over a big rock, express an orange peel over the top, and you've bought yourself half an hour of easy theatre.
