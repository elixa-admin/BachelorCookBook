# Doro Wat

> Ethiopia's great communal stew — chicken braised low in a deep-red berbere sauce, built
> almost entirely from patiently cooked-down onion. It looks like it took all day because,
> in a sense, it did: the onions do the real work, long before the spice or the chicken
> ever hits the pot. Serve it in the middle of the table with injera or flatbread and let
> people tear in.

## Ingredients

- 4 red onions, finely diced
- 60g butter (or niter kibbeh, if you can find it)
- 3 tbsp berbere spice blend
- 3 garlic cloves, grated, 1 tbsp ginger, grated
- 8 chicken thighs and drumsticks, skin removed
- 250ml chicken stock
- 4 hard-boiled eggs, peeled
- Injera or flatbread, to serve

*Rule of thumb: one thigh-and-drumstick pair per person; the onion base and spice
quantities hold steady up to six.*

## Steps

1. Cook the onions in a dry pot, or with the barest film of oil, over low heat for 20–25
   minutes, stirring often, until they collapse down to almost nothing and turn deep gold.
   Don't rush this — it's most of the flavour in the dish.
2. Add the butter, berbere, garlic, and ginger. Cook out the spice paste for 3–4 minutes
   until fragrant and a shade darker.
3. Add the chicken, turning to coat in the paste. Pour in the stock, bring to a simmer.
4. Cover and cook 35–40 minutes, until the chicken is tender and the sauce has thickened
   and deepened in colour.
5. Nestle the whole boiled eggs into the sauce for the last 10 minutes to warm through and
   soak up colour.
6. Serve straight from the pot with injera or warm flatbread.

## The move

Cook the onions alone first, low and slow, until there's almost nothing left of them. That
long reduction — not the spice — is where the depth of the dish actually comes from.

## To drink

Ethiopian honey wine (tej) if you can find it, otherwise a fruity red. Non-alcohol:
hibiscus tea (bissap) or ginger beer.
